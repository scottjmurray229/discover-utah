## Discovering Grand Staircase-Escalante

Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument covers 1.7 million acres of southern Utah canyon country between Bryce Canyon and Capitol Reef — an area roughly the size of Delaware with no paved interior roads, no entrance stations, and no crowds. Dinosaur fossil beds have been eroding out of the mudstone for millions of years; paleontologists from around the world work excavation sites here every summer. The slot canyon systems in Coyote Gulch and the Escalante River drainage are technically accessible without permits and completely wild.

The Hole-in-the-Rock Road plunges south from the highway into the monument for 57 miles on packed gravel and sand — the last 5 miles passable only by high-clearance 4WD. The reward at the end is views over Glen Canyon that existed before Lake Powell changed everything. Highway 12 itself, the northern boundary route between Bryce Canyon and Capitol Reef, is ranked among the most scenic drives in America — the stretch over the Hogback ridge between Escalante and Boulder drops away into canyon views on both sides simultaneously.

This is serious desert country. Carry more water than you think you need, download offline maps before you leave cell range, and let someone know your route.
